The Illusion of
User‑Centered Design

What if the thing we’ve been taught to prioritize… is also limiting us?

0205:00THE QUOTE

“If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses.”

— HENRY FORD

Ford almost certainly never said this. Historians can’t find it anywhere before the 1950s, long after he died. We keep repeating it because it names something real: people describe the future using the vocabulary of the present.

0307:00THE QUESTION

Can users describe a future they’ve never experienced?

0409:00THE TIMELINE

LET’S TEST THAT QUESTION AGAINST REALITY

How many users asked for exactly this?

  • WALKMAN1979“I hate carrying a stack of cassettes.”
  • IPHONE2007“I want fewer devices, not more.”
  • AIRBNB2008“I don’t trust strangers’ spare rooms.”
  • UBER2009“I wish taxis showed up.”
  • NETFLIX2007“I’m sick of late fees.”

Almost none. What they actually said was smaller than that. Just complaints about the present, never a description of the solution. They described friction. Designers converted friction into form.

0512:00BIG IDEA Nº1

BIG IDEA Nº1

Users are experts in problems.
Designers are experts in possibilities.

Two different kinds of expertise. Conflating them is where design processes quietly go wrong.

0614:00PROBLEM ≠ SOLUTION

USERS OWN THIS

Problem

Research discovers reality: what’s broken, what hurts, what people are settling for.

DESIGNERS OWN THIS

Solution

Design imagines alternatives: the things that don’t exist yet.

Treating research output as a finished brief skips the part where design actually happens.

0717:00LIVE EXERCISE

LIVE EXERCISE · 90 SECONDS

How should bicycles become faster?

Turn to the person next to you. If you were surveying regular cyclists on how to make bicycles faster, what would they suggest?

GO.

0819:00COLLECTING ANSWERS

OKAY, SHOUT SOME OUT

The room says:

  • Lighter frames.
  • Better gears.
  • Thinner tires.
  • More aerodynamic shape.
  • Better brakes, for confidence at speed.

Every answer makes the bicycle better. Hold that thought.

0921:00THE REVEAL

NOTICE WHAT NOBODY SAID

Nobody said: add an engine.

No motorcycle. No e‑bike. No rocket booster. Not because they’re bad ideas. Because the question anchored you inside the category of bicycle. Ask people how to improve a thing, and you get improvements to that thing. Almost never a different thing entirely.

Ask “how can we make checkout faster” and you’ll get a faster checkout. You won’t get “eliminate checkout as a step.” Users answer inside the frame they’re given.

1024:00BIG IDEA Nº2

BIG IDEA Nº2

Research is descriptive.
Design is generative.

The answer is not hiding in the transcripts. Research narrows the possibility space. It doesn’t hand you the design.

1127:00THE PROCESS

Research touches only the edges.

Observe → Interpret → Imagine → Prototype THIS IS WHERE DESIGN LIVES → Validate

The middle three stages don’t show up in a research report. They’re also the hardest to defend in a stakeholder meeting, because you can’t point to a user quote to justify them.

1230:00LIVE MOMENT

LIVE MOMENT · SHOW OF HANDS

When did designers become afraid of intuition?

“That’s just your opinion. Do you have data for that?”

A gut feeling is a guess. Expert intuition is pattern recognition.

1335:00PATTERNS

“Nobody asked for this.”

People said: “I can’t find new music I’ll actually like.”

A vague, emotional complaint, turned into a specific, structural solution. The need was user‑sourced. The mechanism was designer‑imagined.

1439:00THE DANGER

THE RISK, IF WE SKIP ALL OF THIS

“What do you want?”

“What are you trying to achieve?”

Ask people what they want, and we optimize yesterday. Small, safe, incremental improvements to a thing that may not need to exist in its current form at all.

1542:00BIG IDEA Nº3

BIG IDEA Nº3

User‑inspired.
Not user‑directed.

Grounded in real pain, real behavior, real constraints. Not handing the steering wheel to a feature-request list and calling it a roadmap.

1644:00LIVE MOMENT

PLACE YOURSELF ON THIS LINE

Where did your last project actually sit?

Research Vision
TOO FAR → INCREMENTAL INNOVATION TOO FAR → PRODUCTS NOBODY WANTS

Not every project wants the same ratio. A checkout flow sits near research. A brand‑new category needs vision.

The mistake isn’t picking a point on the line. It’s not realizing you’re allowed to move along it.

1748:00THE CLOSE

UCD NEVER MEANT “LET USERS DESIGN THE PRODUCT.”

Users reveal reality.

Designers reveal possibility.

Our job was never to choose between them. It’s to hold both at once.
